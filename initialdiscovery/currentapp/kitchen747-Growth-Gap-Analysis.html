How This Works

Most businesses solve the wrong problem.

They invest in SEO when their real issue is conversion. They redesign their website when their real issue is local search visibility. They pay agencies thousands of dollars to optimize things that aren't actually broken — while the actual problem goes unaddressed.

This analysis exists to solve that. Before you spend a dollar on optimization, you need to know which half of your growth problem actually exists — and exactly where the gap is.

Pillar 1 — Local Search Visibility

How many people can find you when they're actively searching for what you offer? 75%+ of all clicks in local searches go to the top 3 results in Google's Map Pack. If your business isn't in those positions, a competitor captures that customer — every search, every time.

Pillar 2 — Conversion

When someone finds you and clicks through, does your website turn them into a customer? 70% of local searches happen on mobile. A site that's slow, missing a booking path, or lacking trust signals loses the majority of visitor intent before any action is taken.

Low Effort Critical Impact
Why It Matters

Your GBP primary category is the #1 ranking factor for your Maps listing. It tells Google which searches you're eligible to appear in. The wrong category — even a close one — suppresses Map Pack eligibility for your core keywords.

How Google Measures It

Google matches your primary category against searcher query intent. A more specific and accurate category produces a stronger relevance signal for the keywords you actually want to rank for.

How to Fix It
  • Log into Google Business Profile Manager (business.google.com)
  • Go to Edit Profile → Business Category
  • Search for the most specific category that describes your core service
  • Set it as Primary — avoid overly broad categories
  • Add 2–3 secondary categories for supporting services
  • Allow 24–48 hours for Google to process
02
GBP Secondary Categories — Expand Your Ranking Surface
GBP_SECONDARY_CATEGORIES
Low Effort High Impact
Why It Matters

Secondary GBP categories tell Google what else your business offers beyond its primary service. Each additional category makes your listing eligible to rank for a wider set of search queries. The benchmark is 3+ — businesses with fewer secondary categories compete in a narrower eligibility pool.

How Google Measures It

Google uses your full category set — primary and secondary — to determine which searches your listing qualifies for. A spa with 'Day Spa' as primary but no secondary categories misses eligibility for searches like 'massage near me' or 'facial seattle' that a competitor with secondary categories like 'Massage Therapist' and 'Skin Care Clinic' would capture.

How to Fix It
  • Log into Google Business Profile Manager (business.google.com)
  • Go to Edit Profile → Business Category
  • Below your primary category, click 'Add another category'
  • Add categories for each major service you offer (e.g., if primary is 'Day Spa', add 'Massage Therapist', 'Skin Care Clinic', 'Nail Salon')
  • Target 3+ secondary categories — the benchmark for full credit
  • Save changes and allow 24–48 hours for Google to process

Why It Matters

GBP posts signal to Google that your listing is actively managed — a factor that has grown in weight in the 2026 ranking model. Zero posts looks abandoned compared to competitors posting weekly.

How Google Measures It

Google tracks post recency and frequency as engagement signals. Posts containing your service keywords and city name also reinforce your category relevance signal. Benchmark: at least one post per week.

How to Fix It

Review Response Rate
REVIEW_RESPONSE_RATE_LOW
Low Effort High Impact
Why It Matters

A low response rate signals an unmanaged business to both Google and every potential customer reading your reviews. Owner responses are a direct engagement signal in GBP's prominence scoring.

How Google Measures It

Google tracks response rate and recency as GBP engagement signals. High response rate indicates an active, customer-focused business — contributing to local prominence scoring.

How to Fix It
  • Set a weekly reminder to check and respond to all new reviews

06
Review Count — Total Review Volume
REVIEW_COUNT_LOW
Medium Effort High Impact
Why It Matters

Total review volume is one of Google's top three local ranking signals. A business with more reviews signals to Google that it has served more customers, accumulated more trust over time, and is more established in the market. In a Map Pack tie — two businesses with similar GBP health and proximity — review count is frequently the tiebreaker. Volume and velocity work together: velocity keeps your profile active month to month, but volume is what gives your profile authority.

How Google Measures It

Google reads total review count directly from your GBP listing as part of the Prominence signal. Unlike velocity (which decays), total count compounds — every review you earn stays on your record permanently.

How to Fix It
  • Build a repeatable review ask into your post-service workflow — immediately after the job is complete is when satisfaction is highest
  • Text-based requests outperform email by 3-5x — a simple text with a direct Google review link is the highest-converting format
  • Add your Google review link to receipts, invoices, follow-up emails, and your email signature
  • Check if your booking or field service platform (Jobber, ServiceTitan, Mindbody, etc.) has built-in review request automation
  • Consistent monthly asks compound faster than occasional pushes — 3 per month for 12 months outperforms 36 in one month
  • Never incentivize reviews — violates Google policy and risks GBP suspension

Medium Effort Medium Impact
Why It Matters

Unused JavaScript is downloaded and parsed on every page load with zero benefit — adding to load time and Total Blocking Time. At high levels it creates a meaningful drag on PageSpeed score.

How Google Measures It

PageSpeed Insights flags unused JS under 'Reduce unused JavaScript' with a time-cost estimate. TBT reflects how long the browser is blocked processing JS before it can respond to user input.

How to Fix It

  • Remove any third-party scripts, plugins, or integrations no longer in use
  • Defer non-critical JS using async/defer attributes where possible
  • If using a website builder, disable any unused built-in features that inject scripts

CLS_HIGH
Medium Effort Medium Impact
Why It Matters

Cumulative Layout Shift measures how much page elements move during load. High CLS means content jumps around as the page loads — a poor user experience that Google now factors directly into rankings.

How Google Measures It

CLS is a Core Web Vital with a direct ranking impact. Google benchmarks: ≤0.05 Good, 0.05–0.10 Needs Improvement, >0.10 Poor.

How to Fix It
  • Set explicit width and height attributes on all images and video embeds
  • Avoid inserting content above existing content after page load
  • Use font-display: swap for web fonts to prevent layout shifts during font loading
  • Test with PageSpeed Insights or Chrome DevTools → Rendering → Layout Shift Regions
